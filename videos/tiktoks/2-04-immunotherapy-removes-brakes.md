**Full voiceover:**
"Checkpoint inhibitors changed oncology because they do something unusual. They usually do not poison the cancer cell directly. They release immune brakes. Tumors can exploit pathways like PD-1 and PD-L1 to tell T cells to stand down. A checkpoint inhibitor blocks that false stop signal. When it works, the immune system recognizes and attacks the cancer. When it fails, the problem may be antigen presentation, immune exclusion, suppressive microenvironment, or the wrong tumor biology. Immunotherapy is powerful because it recruits a living system, and unpredictable for the same reason."
