The most powerful cancer drug is the one that keeps a cell from ever becoming cancer.

Hold the comparison. Modern oncology can extend life dramatically in many cancers — pembrolizumab in melanoma, imatinib in CML, osimertinib in EGFR-mutant lung cancer, venetoclax-azacitidine in AML. The cost is high. The drugs are administered for months or years, with significant toxicities, and they typically buy time rather than producing cures. The patient lives longer, sometimes much longer, but is often living with chronic disease and chronic therapy.

Now compare with prevention. The HPV vaccine, administered to a 12-year-old in three injections costing perhaps $200 total, prevents virtually all cervical cancer and most HPV-driven oropharyngeal cancer that would otherwise have occurred decades later. The hepatitis B vaccine, similarly, prevents most HBV-driven hepatocellular carcinoma. Tobacco cessation prevents roughly 30 percent of all cancer deaths in the United States. Aspirin, in selected patients, reduces colorectal cancer mortality by 20-30 percent. Weight loss, exercise, diet modification — each prevents some cancer.

The math of prevention is staggering. The American Cancer Society estimates that roughly half of all cancer deaths in the United States are attributable to modifiable risk factors. Of the global cancer burden, the WHO estimates that 30-50 percent is preventable through interventions we already know about — interventions that, in aggregate, would prevent more cancer deaths than every cancer drug ever invented combined.

Yet prevention often loses funding battles. Cancer research dollars flow disproportionately to treatment development. Health systems are paid for treating disease, not for preventing it. Patients seek miracle cures rather than behavioral changes. The political economy of cancer favors the dramatic over the quiet. This is one of the most consequential mismatches in health policy.

This chapter is the first of two on cancer prevention and screening. This one — 17A — covers *primary prevention*: lifestyle modification, vaccination, chemoprevention, and the structural interventions that prevent cancer from starting in the first place. The next — 17B — covers *secondary prevention*: screening programs designed to catch cancer at early, curable stages. The split is principled. Primary prevention prevents the disease; secondary prevention catches the disease early enough to cure it.

Hold the question: *given what we know about how cancer develops, where is the cheapest, most effective place to interrupt the process?*

## The framework: levels of prevention

Public health distinguishes three levels of prevention.

*Primary prevention* — preventing the disease from occurring. For cancer: not smoking, not getting infected with oncogenic viruses, not being exposed to environmental carcinogens, maintaining healthy weight, eating healthful diets, not drinking heavily, not being exposed to excessive sun.

*Secondary prevention* — detecting the disease at an early stage when it is more treatable. For cancer: screening tests like mammography, colonoscopy, low-dose CT for lung cancer, Pap smears, PSA testing.

*Tertiary prevention* — managing established disease to prevent complications and progression. For cancer: standard treatment, adjuvant therapy, monitoring for recurrence.

This chapter focuses on primary prevention. The next covers secondary prevention. Tertiary prevention is essentially everything else in this book.

The framework matters because the interventions and the populations differ. Primary prevention targets healthy people — the intervention must be very safe and very cheap to be cost-effective when applied at population scale. Secondary prevention targets asymptomatic people at elevated risk — the screening test must perform well at distinguishing cancer from non-cancer to justify its costs and risks. Tertiary prevention targets people known to have cancer — the intervention can be more aggressive because the disease is already present.

## Tobacco: the single biggest opportunity

Tobacco is the dominant modifiable cause of cancer in the modern world. Tobacco causes about 22 percent of cancer deaths globally and roughly 30 percent in high-income countries. The carcinogen profile of tobacco smoke (Chapter 8A) drives cancers throughout the body — lung most notably, but also oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, esophagus, stomach, pancreas, kidney, bladder, cervix, and acute myeloid leukemia.

The number of cancers that would not occur if tobacco use were eliminated is staggering. In the United States, lung cancer alone kills about 140,000 people annually; about 90 percent of those deaths are tobacco-related. Eliminating tobacco would prevent roughly 130,000 lung cancer deaths per year in the US, plus tens of thousands of additional deaths from tobacco-related cancers in other tissues. This represents a larger annual reduction in cancer mortality than every cancer therapy ever developed combined.

Primary prevention of tobacco-related cancer happens at three levels.

*Preventing initiation*. Young people who never start smoking will not become smokers as adults. The interventions include comprehensive tobacco-control policies — high tobacco taxes (one of the most consistently effective tobacco-control measures), restrictions on advertising and marketing, plain packaging, age-of-sale restrictions, smoke-free environments, school-based prevention programs, and graphic warning labels. Countries that have implemented comprehensive tobacco-control policies (Australia, Norway, the UK) have seen substantial declines in adolescent smoking initiation.

*Promoting cessation*. Adult smokers who quit reduce their cancer risk. Risk declines slowly — never returning to never-smoker baseline, but approaching it after 15-20 years of abstinence. Cessation interventions include behavioral counseling, pharmacotherapy (nicotine replacement therapy, varenicline, bupropion), and emerging options like e-cigarettes (controversial — they are clearly less harmful than combusted tobacco, but also clearly not harmless, and their role in cessation versus initiation is contested).

*Reducing harm in continued users*. Filtered cigarettes were once thought to reduce harm but didn't. Low-tar cigarettes similarly. The product modifications that have actually reduced harm are switching from combustible tobacco to nicotine delivery systems that don't burn — nicotine replacement therapy, e-cigarettes, heated tobacco products. The harm reduction in these alternatives is real but the population-level effects depend on uptake patterns.

The global tobacco picture is uneven. Smoking rates have declined substantially in many high-income countries (the US, the UK, Australia, much of Western Europe). They have risen or remained high in many low- and middle-income countries, where tobacco companies have shifted their marketing. The cancer burden is shifting accordingly. Effective tobacco control is one of the highest-value public health interventions available, and the gap between countries that have implemented it well and those that haven't is one of the largest predictable contributors to global cancer disparities.

The clinician's role in tobacco cessation is well-established. Brief advice from a physician to quit smoking, even a few minutes of focused intervention, increases quit rates measurably. Combined with referral to cessation programs and pharmacotherapy, the effect is substantial. Every primary care visit should include tobacco assessment and cessation counseling for smokers. Most don't.

## Vaccination: prevention by infection control

A small but consequential fraction of cancer is caused by infections — about 13 percent globally, with higher proportions in low- and middle-income countries. Most infection-related cancer can be prevented by vaccination or treatment of the underlying infection (Chapter 8B).

*HPV vaccination* is the most powerful cancer-prevention vaccine. The vaccine targets the major oncogenic HPV types — HPV-16 and HPV-18 in the original quadrivalent vaccine, plus five additional high-risk types in the current 9-valent vaccine. The vaccine is most effective when administered before exposure to HPV, typically at age 11-12 (with catch-up available through age 26).

The data on HPV vaccine effectiveness are remarkable. In countries with widespread vaccination, the prevalence of vaccine-targeted HPV types in young women has dropped 80-90 percent. Cervical cancer rates in vaccinated cohorts are dropping. Australia, with universal HPV vaccination of adolescents since 2007 and high coverage rates, is projected to effectively eliminate cervical cancer as a public health problem in the coming decades.

The barriers to global HPV vaccination are mainly access and cost. The vaccine is expensive (about $200-300 per three-dose course in commercial markets, lower in low-income countries through Gavi and other programs). Implementation requires school-based vaccination programs and reliable cold chains. Public hesitancy — sometimes related to misinformation about safety — has limited uptake in some countries. The US has lagged substantially behind Australia and the UK in coverage, with consequences that will play out in cancer incidence over coming decades.

*Hepatitis B vaccination* prevents most HBV-driven hepatocellular carcinoma. The vaccine has been available since 1982 and is now part of the WHO-recommended universal childhood immunization schedule. Countries with widespread coverage have seen dramatic drops in chronic hepatitis B infection rates and in associated liver cancer. The vaccine has been called "the first cancer-preventing vaccine."

Global HBV vaccination coverage exceeds 80 percent of infants in many countries, but pockets of low coverage remain in low-income settings. The integration of HBV vaccination into routine infant immunization schedules has been one of the most successful global health interventions of the past 40 years.

*Hepatitis C* doesn't have a vaccine but is curable with direct-acting antivirals. Treatment of chronic HCV with the modern drugs (sofosbuvir, ledipasvir, glecaprevir/pibrentasvir, others) achieves cure rates exceeding 95 percent in most populations. Cured patients have substantially reduced liver cancer risk, though residual risk persists in those who developed cirrhosis before treatment. Aggressive screening for HCV and treatment of identified cases is recommended in many guidelines.

*Helicobacter pylori* eradication is the third major infection-related cancer prevention strategy. *H. pylori* causes the majority of gastric cancer (Chapter 8B). One-to-two-week courses of antibiotics (proton pump inhibitor plus two antibiotics) cure most infections. Japan implemented a national *H. pylori* eradication program in 2013, with gradual implementation across the population. Cancer prevention through this approach is being demonstrated; the long latency of *H. pylori*-driven gastric cancer means full effects will take decades to play out.

## Diet, weight, and physical activity

The relationships between diet, body weight, physical activity, and cancer risk are detailed in Chapter 3. The interventions for primary prevention focus on:

*Maintaining healthy body weight*. Obesity is now causally linked to at least 13 cancers and is the second most important modifiable cancer risk factor after tobacco in many high-income countries. The interventions: maintaining or achieving healthy weight through diet and physical activity, treating obesity medically when behavioral interventions are insufficient (the GLP-1 receptor agonists like semaglutide and tirzepatide are dramatically effective for weight loss and may indirectly reduce cancer risk; bariatric surgery for severe obesity reduces several cancers in long-term follow-up studies).

*Physical activity*. Regular physical activity reduces risk of colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, kidney, esophageal, and bladder cancers. WHO recommendations call for at least 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity activity weekly. The effects are dose-dependent and consistent across observational studies.

*Healthful dietary patterns*. Mediterranean diets and DASH-style diets are associated with reduced overall cancer mortality. Specific food categories: fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes (associated with reduced risk); processed meat (associated with increased risk, classified as Group 1 carcinogen for colorectal cancer); red meat (associated with modest risk elevation); ultra-processed foods (associated with various adverse outcomes including cancer).

*Limited alcohol use*. Alcohol is causally linked to mouth, throat, esophagus, liver, breast, and colorectal cancers. Even moderate consumption raises breast cancer risk. The "alcohol is good for the heart" framing has been challenged; cancer harms appear to outweigh putative cardiovascular benefits across most contexts. Public health recommendations are increasingly toward limiting alcohol.

The challenge of these interventions is that behavior change is hard. Telling people to lose weight, exercise more, eat differently, and drink less doesn't work very well at population scale, even though the recommendations are biologically sound. Effective interventions usually require structural changes — built environment that supports physical activity, food policy that makes healthful options affordable and accessible, taxation and pricing that disincentivizes harmful products, regulatory changes that affect food industry behavior.

## Sun exposure and UV protection

Skin cancers — basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma — are largely preventable through sun protection. Total elimination is impractical (people need some sun for vitamin D synthesis, and outdoor activity is fundamental to human well-being), but the harms can be substantially reduced.

The interventions: avoiding peak sun hours (typically 10 AM to 4 PM in most temperate latitudes), using sun-protective clothing (long sleeves, hats with brims, UV-blocking sunglasses), applying broad-spectrum sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher), avoiding tanning beds (which deliver intense UV-A and UV-B and are classified as Group 1 carcinogens by IARC).

The behavioral component is well-studied. Public health campaigns ("Slip, Slop, Slap" in Australia) have demonstrably reduced sun-protection behaviors. The combination of cultural change, regulatory action on tanning beds, and reduced ozone layer (a continuing concern) determine population-level outcomes.

Skin self-examination and dermatologic surveillance are forms of secondary prevention (covered in 17B). For primary prevention, sun protection in childhood and adolescence is particularly impactful — substantial UV-driven mutational accumulation occurs early in life, and the cancer risks decades later are heavily driven by early-life exposure.

## Occupational and environmental exposures

Reducing occupational and environmental exposure to known carcinogens is one of the highest-leverage prevention strategies, but the responsibility falls largely on policy rather than individual behavior.

*Asbestos*. Banned for most uses in developed countries by the 1980s-90s. Still used in some low-income countries; the resulting mesothelioma epidemic is ahead of those populations, not behind. Continued advocacy for global asbestos ban is one of the cleaner public health interventions.

*Tobacco* in occupational settings. Smoke-free workplace laws reduce exposure to secondhand smoke. Compulsory non-smoking in indoor public spaces has been implemented in most high-income countries.

*Industrial chemicals*. Occupational exposure limits for benzene, vinyl chloride, formaldehyde, and other Group 1 carcinogens reduce workers' cancer risks. Regulatory infrastructure (OSHA in the US, equivalent agencies elsewhere) enforces these limits with variable rigor.

*Air pollution*. Outdoor air pollution (particularly PM2.5) is now an IARC Group 1 carcinogen. Public policy interventions — emission standards, transition to cleaner energy, urban planning that reduces traffic — affect population-level exposure. The benefits are diffuse but enormous in aggregate.

*Radon*. Naturally occurring radioactive gas; second leading cause of lung cancer in the US after smoking. Home radon testing is cheap. Mitigation, when elevated levels are found, is moderately expensive (typically $1,000-2,500) but one-time. Public health programs to encourage testing exist but are underutilized.

*Indoor cooking pollution*. In low-income countries where wood, dung, or coal are burned indoors for cooking, women and children are exposed to high levels of particulates and PAHs. Lung cancer rates in non-smoking women in these settings are elevated. Transitioning to cleaner fuels (LPG, electricity) is one of the most cost-effective health interventions globally.

The pattern across environmental and occupational carcinogens is that regulation is typically more effective than individual behavior. Asking workers to "be careful" with benzene doesn't work as well as setting exposure limits and requiring engineering controls. Asking individuals to choose unpolluted air doesn't work as well as setting air quality standards.

## Chemoprevention

A separate category of primary prevention is *chemoprevention* — the use of drugs to prevent cancer in people at elevated risk. The evidence base is significant for several agents in specific populations.

*Aspirin* and colorectal cancer prevention. Long-term low-dose aspirin (75-325 mg/day) reduces colorectal cancer incidence and mortality by about 20-30 percent in long-term users. The mechanism is likely COX-2 inhibition reducing prostaglandin-driven inflammation and proliferation. The benefit applies to other cancers (esophageal, stomach, possibly more) but is best established for colorectal.

The current recommendations are nuanced. The US Preventive Services Task Force previously recommended aspirin for primary prevention of colorectal cancer in adults 50-69 with elevated cardiovascular risk. In 2022, the recommendation was reduced — aspirin is no longer recommended for primary prevention starting at age 60 due to bleeding risk. The benefit-risk calculation depends on age, baseline cardiovascular risk, and baseline cancer risk. For high-risk populations (Lynch syndrome carriers, people with a strong family history of colorectal cancer), aspirin is more clearly beneficial.

*Tamoxifen* and *raloxifene* for breast cancer prevention. Selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) reduce breast cancer incidence in women at elevated risk (5-year Gail model risk ≥1.7%). The reductions are substantial — about 50 percent reduction in invasive breast cancer over 5 years of therapy in trial populations. The drugs have side effects (hot flashes, thromboembolic events, endometrial cancer with tamoxifen) that limit adoption.

*Aromatase inhibitors* (exemestane, anastrozole) for breast cancer prevention in postmenopausal women at elevated risk. More effective than tamoxifen at preventing breast cancer but with different toxicity profile (musculoskeletal symptoms, bone loss).

*Finasteride* for prostate cancer prevention. The 5-α reductase inhibitor reduces prostate cancer incidence in men over 55, though with some controversy about whether the cancers prevented are the indolent ones (irrelevant clinically) while the aggressive ones may be slightly elevated.

*HPV-related vaccines* for cervical cancer prevention, already covered.

*COX-2 inhibitors* for various cancer prevention contexts. Strong preclinical and observational data; mixed clinical trial data; substantial cardiovascular toxicity concerns from the rofecoxib era have limited enthusiasm.

The general pattern of chemoprevention is that it works in identifiable high-risk populations but has side effects that limit broader adoption. Risk stratification — identifying who would benefit most — is increasingly important. Genetic risk scoring and biomarker-based risk assessment are improving the ability to target chemoprevention to those most likely to benefit.

## Genetic counseling and risk reduction

For individuals with hereditary cancer predisposition syndromes, primary prevention takes specialized forms.

*BRCA1/2 mutation carriers* face dramatically elevated breast and ovarian cancer risks (lifetime risks of 65-80% for breast cancer, 40-60% for ovarian). Prevention options include intensive screening (more on this in 17B), chemoprevention (tamoxifen), and prophylactic surgery (bilateral mastectomy reducing breast cancer risk by 90%+, bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy reducing ovarian cancer risk substantially and breast cancer risk modestly). The decisions involve substantial trade-offs — surgery has its own consequences. Genetic counseling is essential.

*Lynch syndrome* (HNPCC) carriers face elevated risks for colorectal (50-80%), endometrial (40-60%), and several other cancers. Prevention focuses on intensive colonoscopy screening (every 1-2 years starting at age 20-25), gynecologic surveillance, and prophylactic hysterectomy with bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy after childbearing is complete in women.

*Familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)*. Lifetime risk of colorectal cancer is essentially 100% if untreated. Prevention requires prophylactic colectomy in adolescence or early adulthood. Surveillance for upper GI tumors, thyroid, and other associated cancers continues lifelong.

*Multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2 (MEN2)*. Germline RET mutations. Prophylactic thyroidectomy in early childhood for high-risk variants. Ongoing surveillance for pheochromocytoma and parathyroid disease.

*Li-Fraumeni syndrome*. Germline TP53 mutations. Lifetime cancer risk >90%; many tumor types, often starting in childhood. Comprehensive surveillance approaches with whole-body MRI and other modalities being studied. No prophylactic surgical options across all the diverse cancer sites.

*Genetic testing* for cancer susceptibility has become increasingly accessible. Multi-gene panels covering 20-100+ cancer-associated genes are now standard for testing in suspected hereditary cancer cases. Direct-to-consumer tests have raised both access and quality concerns. Reflexive testing in cancer patients (testing newly diagnosed patients to identify hereditary cases for family screening) is increasingly recommended.

## What this chapter gives you

Primary prevention is the highest-leverage intervention in cancer control. The opportunities exist at multiple levels.

*Tobacco control* prevents the single largest fraction of preventable cancer — about 30 percent of cancer deaths in high-income countries are tobacco-attributable. Comprehensive tobacco control policies (taxation, marketing restrictions, smoke-free environments, cessation support) have dramatically reduced smoking in many countries and would prevent millions of deaths globally if implemented broadly.

*Vaccination* against oncogenic viruses prevents 13% of cancers globally. HPV vaccines, hepatitis B vaccines, and (through treatment) hepatitis C are the major opportunities. Coverage gaps in low- and middle-income countries represent enormous preventable cancer burden.

*Lifestyle factors* — body weight, physical activity, diet, alcohol — each contribute to cancer risk and to preventable burden. Behavioral interventions at the individual level are limited; structural interventions (built environment, food policy, alcohol regulation) are more effective at scale.

*UV protection* prevents most skin cancers. Sun protection behaviors and regulation of tanning beds are the major interventions.

*Occupational and environmental exposures* to carcinogens are reduced through regulation. Asbestos bans, air pollution standards, occupational exposure limits, and radon mitigation programs are the major examples.

*Chemoprevention* with aspirin, tamoxifen, raloxifene, aromatase inhibitors, and others has evidence for specific populations. The benefit-risk calculation depends on individual risk profile.

*Hereditary cancer risk* requires genetic counseling, intensive surveillance, sometimes prophylactic surgery, and tailored chemoprevention. The diagnostic and management infrastructure for hereditary cancer is expanding.

Chapter 17B covers secondary prevention — screening programs designed to detect cancer at early, curable stages. The interventions and the populations are different, but the goal — reducing cancer mortality through pre-symptomatic detection or intervention — is complementary.
