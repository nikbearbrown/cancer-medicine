# Chapter 18 — Anti-Angiogenic Therapy: Promise and Reality

Folkman predicted that starving tumors of their blood supply would be the great cure. He was half right.

Hold the qualifier. Anti-angiogenic therapy has produced FDA-approved drugs that extend life for patients with several cancers. Bevacizumab, the first VEGF-targeting antibody, was approved in 2004 for metastatic colorectal cancer and has since been approved for ovarian, lung, kidney, and other cancers. The tyrosine kinase inhibitors — sunitinib, sorafenib, pazopanib, axitinib, cabozantinib, lenvatinib — block VEGFR-2 and related kinases and have transformed treatment of renal cell carcinoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, and several other diseases. The drugs work.

But the magnitude of benefit has generally been modest. Progression-free survival benefits in the range of 2-5 months. Overall survival benefits sometimes statistically significant, sometimes not. Few of the responses are complete or durable. Resistance emerges in essentially all patients. The vision of tumor starvation, of cancers melting away as their blood supply is cut off, has not materialized in the clean form Folkman imagined.

Why? Several reasons. Tumors can switch to alternative angiogenic pathways when VEGF is blocked. They can co-opt existing vessels rather than build new ones. They can adapt their metabolism to tolerate hypoxia. They can recruit pro-angiogenic stromal cells that provide redundant signaling. Anti-angiogenic therapy is also, paradoxically, sometimes *too* effective — when vessels regress too aggressively, the tumor becomes hypoxic and selects for the most aggressive, hypoxia-tolerant cells. The vasculature gets worse, not better, for drug delivery.

The conceptual reframing, led by Rakesh Jain and colleagues at Harvard starting in the early 2000s, is the *vascular normalization* hypothesis. The goal of anti-angiogenic therapy may not be to eliminate tumor vasculature but to *make it more normal* — to prune the excess and abnormal vessels while improving the function of the remaining ones. Normalized vessels deliver drugs more efficiently, oxygenate the tumor better, and create a less immunosuppressive microenvironment. This has reframed combination strategies and is now the dominant model for understanding how anti-angiogenic drugs actually work in patients.

This chapter — the second on angiogenesis — covers the clinical translation. Anti-angiogenic drug development, the approved agents, the surprising biology of vascular normalization, the resistance mechanisms, and the combinations with immunotherapy that are reshaping the field.

## Bevacizumab and the VEGF antibody story

Bevacizumab (Avastin) is the prototypical anti-angiogenic drug. It is a humanized monoclonal antibody that binds VEGF-A in the bloodstream and prevents it from binding VEGFR-2 on endothelial cells. By neutralizing the dominant pro-angiogenic ligand, bevacizumab interrupts the central signaling pathway driving tumor angiogenesis.

The drug was developed at Genentech by a team led by Napoleone Ferrara (who had previously isolated and characterized VEGF). The phase 3 trial in metastatic colorectal cancer (Hurwitz et al., *NEJM* 2004) showed that adding bevacizumab to standard chemotherapy (irinotecan-based regimens, then later FOLFOX) extended overall survival by about 5 months — from about 16 months to about 20 months in median. The benefit was modest but statistically significant, and the drug was approved by the FDA in February 2004.

The colorectal cancer success led to expanded development. Bevacizumab is now approved for:
- Metastatic colorectal cancer (with chemotherapy)
- Non-squamous non-small-cell lung cancer (with chemotherapy)
- Recurrent glioblastoma (single agent)
- Metastatic renal cell carcinoma (with interferon)
- Metastatic cervical cancer (with chemotherapy)
- Recurrent ovarian cancer (with chemotherapy)
- Hepatocellular carcinoma (in combination with atezolizumab)

The benefits across these indications have varied. Some are robust (the atezolizumab-bevacizumab combination in hepatocellular carcinoma, IMbrave150 trial — improved overall survival). Others are more modest (glioblastoma — bevacizumab improves progression-free survival but not overall survival). The general pattern is that bevacizumab provides incremental benefit when added to other modalities, rather than producing the dramatic responses seen with some targeted therapies.

The toxicity profile of bevacizumab is informative about VEGF biology in normal tissues. VEGF is required for vascular homeostasis throughout the body, not just in tumors. Side effects include hypertension (from reduced nitric oxide signaling in normal vessels), proteinuria (from compromised glomerular endothelial function in the kidney), poor wound healing, GI perforation (in some patients with bowel involvement), and rarely arterial thrombotic events. The toxicities limit dosing and require careful patient selection.

Aflibercept (ziv-aflibercept) is a related anti-VEGF agent — a recombinant fusion protein containing portions of VEGFR-1 and VEGFR-2 that acts as a "VEGF trap," binding VEGF-A, VEGF-B, and PlGF. It is approved for metastatic colorectal cancer (after first-line FOLFOX failure) and ophthalmologic conditions. Ramucirumab is a monoclonal antibody against VEGFR-2 (rather than VEGF itself) approved for gastric, lung, and other cancers.

## Tyrosine kinase inhibitors

The other major anti-angiogenic strategy is *small-molecule tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs)* that block VEGFR-2 and related kinases. Most are multi-kinase inhibitors hitting VEGFR-2 plus additional targets like PDGFR, FGFR, c-KIT, RET, and others. The multi-target nature affects both efficacy and toxicity.

*Sunitinib* (Sutent, approved 2006) targets VEGFR-1/2/3, PDGFR-α/β, c-KIT, FLT3, RET, and CSF1R. It is approved for renal cell carcinoma, GI stromal tumors (GIST), and pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors. In metastatic clear cell renal cell carcinoma, sunitinib produced response rates around 30 percent and progression-free survival of about 11 months — a dramatic improvement over the prior standard (interferon, with response rates around 5 percent). Sunitinib was a transformative drug for renal cell carcinoma.

*Sorafenib* (Nexavar, approved 2005) targets VEGFR-2, PDGFR-β, c-KIT, FLT3, and notably the RAF kinases (BRAF and CRAF) — though the RAF inhibition is weaker than later-generation BRAF-specific drugs. Sorafenib is approved for hepatocellular carcinoma, renal cell carcinoma, and thyroid cancer.

*Pazopanib*, *axitinib*, *cabozantinib*, *lenvatinib*, *regorafenib*, *vandetanib*, *tivozanib* — the list of multi-kinase inhibitors with anti-angiogenic activity is long. Each has a somewhat different kinase selectivity profile and different approved indications.

The TKIs share certain limitations. They have substantial off-target activity. Their toxicities (hand-foot syndrome, hypertension, diarrhea, hypothyroidism, hepatotoxicity, cardiac effects) reflect the multiple kinases hit and the systemic nature of VEGFR inhibition. They typically produce response rates of 20-40 percent in their primary indications, with median progression-free survivals of 8-12 months, and tend to have more modest overall survival benefits.

The advantage of TKIs over antibodies is oral administration, generally shorter half-lives (allowing dose adjustment for toxicity), and the ability to hit multiple targets simultaneously (potentially addressing the redundancy that single-target therapy faces).

## Vascular normalization

The conceptual breakthrough about anti-angiogenic therapy came from imaging studies. Rakesh Jain's group at the Massachusetts General Hospital used intravital microscopy to watch tumor vasculature respond to anti-angiogenic drugs in real time. What they saw was not vessel destruction. It was vessel *normalization*.

In the first days after starting bevacizumab or similar anti-VEGF therapy, abnormal tumor vessels regress while more mature vessels persist. The remaining vessels become less leaky. They acquire better pericyte coverage. They become more functional — better blood flow, more uniform perfusion, better oxygenation of the tumor. The interstitial fluid pressure drops. Drug penetration into the tumor improves. Hypoxia is reduced.

The window of normalization is transient. With continued anti-angiogenic therapy, more vessels regress, and eventually the tumor becomes severely hypoxic and the vasculature becomes worse for drug delivery, not better. There is a *window of opportunity* — typically days to a few weeks — during which the vasculature is more functional than at baseline. This is the window during which combined chemotherapy or radiation might work best.

The vascular normalization model has several implications:

1. *Combination timing matters*. Chemotherapy or radiation given during the normalization window should be more effective than given before or after. The schedule of anti-angiogenic plus cytotoxic therapy should be designed to exploit this window.

2. *Higher doses of anti-angiogenic are not necessarily better*. Above some threshold, additional anti-angiogenic activity worsens rather than improves the tumor vasculature. The dose-response is not monotonic.

3. *Anti-angiogenic monotherapy may not be the goal*. Single-agent anti-angiogenic therapy that fully eliminates tumor vessels may produce hypoxic, treatment-resistant tumors. Combination with other modalities is essential.

4. *Imaging biomarkers can track normalization*. Dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI, perfusion CT, and other functional imaging methods can detect changes in tumor vascular function and might help guide therapy.

The normalization model has reframed anti-angiogenic therapy from "starve the tumor" to "improve tumor vasculature so other treatments work better." The data on synergy between anti-angiogenic drugs and chemotherapy, radiation, and immunotherapy are increasingly understood through this lens.

## Resistance to anti-angiogenic therapy

Tumors that initially respond to anti-angiogenic therapy almost always progress eventually. The resistance mechanisms are diverse.

*Upregulation of alternative pro-angiogenic factors*. When VEGF is blocked, tumors can compensate by upregulating FGF, PDGF, angiopoietins, or other pro-angiogenic signals. The resistance is conceptually similar to oncogene-driven cancers using alternative pathways to escape kinase inhibition.

*Selection of vessel co-option*. Tumors that infiltrate vascularized normal tissue and co-opt existing vessels are less dependent on new vessel formation. Anti-VEGF therapy may select for cells with this growth pattern.

*Increased pericyte coverage*. Vessels that have matured with substantial pericyte coverage are less dependent on VEGF for survival than newly formed vessels. Tumor vessels that have been pruned to a more mature state by anti-VEGF therapy may then become VEGF-independent.

*Recruitment of bone marrow-derived cells*. Pro-angiogenic myeloid cells (myeloid-derived suppressor cells, M2 macrophages, neutrophils) can be recruited to the tumor and provide angiogenic signaling that bypasses tumor cell VEGF production.

*Cancer stem cells with intrinsic angiogenic capacity*. Some cancer stem cells may have direct vascular differentiation capacity, contributing to vasculogenic mimicry and intrinsic vascular generation.

*Hypoxia-induced selection*. Anti-angiogenic therapy that creates persistent hypoxia selects for hypoxia-tolerant, metabolically adapted cancer cells. These cells often have invasive, metastatic phenotypes. The therapy that was supposed to constrain the tumor may select for its most aggressive variants.

This last mechanism is one of the most concerning. Some preclinical studies and clinical observations have suggested that anti-angiogenic therapy can accelerate metastasis in some contexts — by selecting for cells that survive hypoxia and that have acquired invasive capabilities. The clinical magnitude of this effect is debated, and the overall benefit-risk calculation still favors anti-angiogenic therapy in approved indications, but it is a real consideration.

The resistance mechanisms inform combination strategies. Anti-angiogenic plus chemotherapy (to kill the cells that survive). Anti-angiogenic plus immunotherapy (to exploit the normalized vasculature for immune cell infiltration). Anti-angiogenic with multi-target kinase inhibitors that hit FGF and PDGF as well as VEGF. The combinations have driven the field forward more than monotherapy has.

## Anti-angiogenic therapy plus immunotherapy

The most exciting current direction is combination of anti-angiogenic therapy with immune checkpoint inhibitors. The biological rationale connects several threads.

The tumor microenvironment is immunosuppressive (Chapter 14 covers this in detail). The immunosuppression is partly metabolic (hypoxia, lactate, nutrient depletion as in Chapter 11B) and partly cellular (regulatory T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, tumor-associated macrophages). VEGF itself is immunosuppressive — it directly inhibits dendritic cell maturation, promotes regulatory T cell function, and recruits suppressive myeloid cells.

Anti-angiogenic therapy that normalizes the vasculature should:
1. Reduce hypoxia, which itself is immunosuppressive.
2. Improve immune cell infiltration into the tumor by improving vessel function.
3. Reduce VEGF-mediated direct immunosuppression.
4. Improve drug delivery for immune checkpoint inhibitors.

The combinations have produced clinically meaningful results. *Atezolizumab plus bevacizumab* in hepatocellular carcinoma (IMbrave150) showed improved overall survival compared to sorafenib. *Pembrolizumab plus axitinib* in renal cell carcinoma (KEYNOTE-426) showed improved overall survival compared to sunitinib. *Lenvatinib plus pembrolizumab* in endometrial cancer (KEYNOTE-775) showed improved outcomes in advanced disease. *Cabozantinib plus nivolumab* in renal cell carcinoma (CheckMate 9ER) is another success.

The combinations have become standard of care in several indications. The conceptual framework — using anti-angiogenic therapy to normalize the tumor microenvironment for immunotherapy — has reframed how clinicians think about these drugs. They are not just anti-tumor agents; they are tumor-microenvironment modifiers that enable other therapies to work.

The toxicities of combinations are additive but generally manageable. The benefits in the relevant indications are substantial — improvements in median overall survival of months in metastatic settings where these are meaningful.

## Other vascular-targeted approaches

Beyond direct angiogenesis inhibition, several other vascular-targeting approaches are in development or use.

*Vascular disrupting agents (VDAs)*. Drugs that disrupt established tumor vasculature acutely. Combretastatin and similar tubulin-binding agents cause rapid disassembly of endothelial cell cytoskeletons, vessel collapse, and central tumor necrosis. The strategy is different from anti-angiogenic therapy (which prevents new vessel formation); VDAs destroy existing vessels. They have had clinical activity but limited durable benefit because the tumor periphery (which receives oxygen by diffusion from outside) survives and re-grows.

*Anti-pericyte agents*. Drugs targeting PDGFR-β on pericytes can destabilize vessels. Some multi-kinase inhibitors hit PDGFR as part of their portfolio.

*Targeting endothelial progenitor cells*. Strategies to block bone marrow-derived endothelial cell mobilization and homing to tumors.

*Anti-Tie-2 / Ang-2 agents*. Vanucizumab and similar agents target the angiopoietin-Tie-2 axis. Activity has been modest.

*Endothelial-cell-specific drug delivery*. Antibody-drug conjugates and nanoparticles that target tumor endothelium specifically, delivering cytotoxics to the vessel wall.

*Targeted radiation to vessels*. Stereotactic radiation that targets specific vessels with high precision.

None of these has matched the impact of VEGF-pathway inhibition, but each is an active area of research.

## Where anti-angiogenic therapy stands

A reasonable summary of the field's current state:

*The biology is real*. Tumors do depend on angiogenesis. VEGF signaling is essential for tumor vascularization. Anti-angiogenic therapy does affect tumor vasculature.

*The clinical benefits are real but modest*. Survival benefits of months in metastatic settings, not years. Response rates of 20-40 percent at best. Progression in essentially all patients eventually.

*The vascular normalization model has reframed the field*. Anti-angiogenic therapy is now understood not as tumor starvation but as tumor microenvironment modification. The combination strategies make sense in this framework in ways that single-agent strategies did not.

*Anti-angiogenic plus immunotherapy is the current frontier*. Multiple FDA-approved combinations. Strong biological rationale. Substantial clinical benefits in the right settings.

*Future directions include*: better patient selection (which tumors are vascular-dependent versus vessel-co-opting?), better imaging biomarkers (to identify the normalization window), better combination scheduling, novel targets in the vascular system, and continued exploration of the vascular-immune interface.

Folkman's vision is not fully realized. But the field he founded has produced real, lasting benefits for cancer patients, and the conceptual frameworks it has generated — vascular normalization, the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, the interactions between vasculature and other tumor compartments — have illuminated cancer biology far beyond just blood vessels.

## What this chapter gives you

Anti-angiogenic therapy has produced FDA-approved drugs (bevacizumab, multi-kinase inhibitors like sunitinib and sorafenib, and others) that extend life for patients with several cancers, though the magnitude of benefit is generally modest. The original "starve the tumor" framing has been replaced by the vascular normalization model — anti-angiogenic therapy improves tumor vascular function in a transient window, with implications for combination timing and combination strategies.

Resistance to anti-angiogenic therapy is essentially universal and involves multiple mechanisms — upregulation of alternative pro-angiogenic factors, vessel co-option, increased pericyte coverage, recruitment of pro-angiogenic myeloid cells, and hypoxia-induced selection for aggressive phenotypes.

The current frontier is combination of anti-angiogenic therapy with immune checkpoint inhibitors. Multiple combinations (atezolizumab+bevacizumab, pembrolizumab+axitinib, lenvatinib+pembrolizumab, cabozantinib+nivolumab) have produced clinically meaningful improvements in overall survival in metastatic settings and have become standards of care.

The conceptual integration with the rest of cancer biology — vasculature as part of the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, metabolism-vasculature feedback through HIF-VEGF, vasculature as a determinant of drug delivery and immune cell infiltration — is one of the success stories of the past decade.

Chapter 13 turns to metastasis and invasion — the processes by which cancer cells leave the primary tumor and establish colonies in distant organs. The biology connects to angiogenesis (metastatic cells need vasculature too), to EMT (the cellular state change that enables invasion), and to the metabolic and immunological topics covered in chapters 11 and 14.
