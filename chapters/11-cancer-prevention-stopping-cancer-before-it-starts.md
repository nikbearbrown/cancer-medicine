# Cancer Prevention: Stopping Cancer Before It Starts

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- **Distinguish** primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention, and **classify** a given intervention into the correct level by what stage of disease it targets.
- **Explain** why tobacco control and oncogenic-virus vaccination are the highest-leverage cancer-prevention interventions, citing the causal evidence (Doll & Hill, 1954).
- **Evaluate** the benefit–risk trade-off in chemoprevention, using the narrowing of aspirin recommendations as a worked case (USPSTF, 2022).
- **Analyze** why population-scale primary prevention demands interventions that are very safe and very cheap, and **predict** which strategies fail when shifted from policy to individual behavior.
- **Produce** a risk-stratified prevention plan for a hereditary cancer syndrome, justifying the trade-offs of surveillance versus prophylactic surgery.

## Opening Case

A health system runs two clinics in the same city. The oncology clinic treats a 58-year-old woman with metastatic cervical cancer; her care over the next two years — surgery, chemoradiation, immunotherapy, supportive care — will cost several hundred thousand dollars and, statistically, buy her time rather than a cure. Down the hall, a pediatric clinic offers the HPV vaccine to 12-year-olds; the series costs roughly $200 and prevents virtually all of the cancer the woman in oncology now has. The woman was never vaccinated — the vaccine did not exist when she was twelve, and by the time it did, her county's uptake had stalled amid cost barriers and hesitancy.

Here is the uncomfortable arithmetic the case forces. The expensive, dramatic intervention extends one life modestly. The cheap, quiet one would have prevented the disease entirely — and the second clinic's waiting room is half empty while the first is full. The American Cancer Society estimates that roughly half of all U.S. cancer deaths are attributable to modifiable risk factors, and the WHO estimates 30–50% of the global cancer burden is preventable with tools we already have. This chapter asks: given how cancer develops, where is the cheapest, most effective place to interrupt the process — and why do we so consistently underinvest there?

## Core Concepts

### Three levels of prevention

Public health sorts prevention by *what stage of disease it targets.*

- **Primary prevention** stops the disease from ever occurring: not smoking, not acquiring oncogenic infections, avoiding carcinogen exposure, maintaining healthy weight, limiting alcohol and sun.
- **Secondary prevention** detects disease at an early, more curable stage: mammography, colonoscopy, low-dose CT for lung cancer, Pap smears. (This chapter's companion covers screening.)
- **Tertiary prevention** manages established disease to prevent complications and progression — essentially the rest of oncology.

The level dictates the constraints. Primary prevention targets *healthy people*, so the intervention must be extremely safe and cheap to justify applying it at population scale — you are intervening on millions who would never have gotten cancer anyway. Secondary prevention targets asymptomatic at-risk people, so the test must reliably separate cancer from non-cancer. Tertiary prevention targets people known to have cancer, so it can be aggressive because the disease is already present. Confusing these levels — applying a treatment-grade risk to a healthy population — is a recurring policy error.

### Tobacco: the single largest opportunity

Tobacco is the dominant modifiable cause of cancer worldwide — about 22% of cancer deaths globally and roughly 30% in high-income countries. The causal case was settled by the British Doctors Study, the first large prospective cohort to make a definitive causal argument for a behavioral cancer risk factor (Doll & Hill, 1954); before it, the smoking–lung-cancer link rested on weaker case-control data. Tobacco smoke drives cancer far beyond the lung — oral cavity, larynx, esophagus, pancreas, bladder, kidney, cervix, and acute myeloid leukemia among them.

The scale is hard to overstate. In the U.S., lung cancer kills about 140,000 people a year, roughly 90% tobacco-related; eliminating tobacco would prevent more annual cancer deaths than every cancer therapy ever developed, combined. Primary prevention here operates at three points: **preventing initiation** in young people (high tobacco taxes — among the most consistently effective measures — plus advertising restrictions, plain packaging, age-of-sale limits, and smoke-free laws); **promoting cessation** in adults (counseling plus pharmacotherapy — nicotine replacement, varenicline, bupropion); and **harm reduction** in continued users (switching from combusted tobacco to non-combusted nicotine, where the benefit is real but population effects depend on uptake patterns). Even brief physician advice to quit measurably raises quit rates — a high-value intervention that most primary-care visits still skip.

### Vaccination: preventing the infections that cause cancer

About 13% of cancer globally is caused by infection, and most of that is preventable by vaccination or by treating the infection.

**HPV vaccination** is the most powerful cancer-prevention vaccine. The current 9-valent vaccine targets the major oncogenic HPV types and is most effective given *before* exposure, around age 11–12. In countries with high coverage, the prevalence of vaccine-targeted HPV types in young women has dropped 80–90%, and cervical-cancer rates in vaccinated cohorts are falling. Australia, with universal adolescent vaccination since 2007 and high coverage, is projected to effectively eliminate cervical cancer as a public-health problem in the coming decades. The barriers are access, cost, and hesitancy — and the U.S. has lagged, with consequences that will surface in incidence over decades.

**Hepatitis B vaccination** — available since 1982 and called "the first cancer-preventing vaccine" — prevents most HBV-driven hepatocellular carcinoma; its integration into routine infant immunization is one of the great global-health successes of the past 40 years. **Hepatitis C** has no vaccine but is now curable with direct-acting antivirals (cure rates above 95%), sharply reducing later liver-cancer risk. **Helicobacter pylori** eradication with a short antibiotic course prevents gastric cancer; Japan implemented a national eradication program in 2013, though the long latency means full effects will take decades.

### Lifestyle: real effects, hard to capture at scale

Body weight, physical activity, diet, and alcohol each contribute meaningfully to cancer risk. **Obesity** is causally linked to at least 13 cancers and is the second-ranking modifiable risk factor after tobacco in many high-income countries. **Physical activity** (WHO recommends 150–300 minutes/week of moderate activity) reduces colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, and other cancers. **Diet** matters at the pattern level (Mediterranean and DASH-style diets associate with lower cancer mortality) and the component level — processed meat is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen for colorectal cancer. **Alcohol** is causally linked to cancers of the mouth, throat, esophagus, liver, breast, and colorectum, with even moderate intake raising breast-cancer risk.

The hard truth is that behavior change is weak at population scale. Telling people to eat better, move more, and drink less is biologically sound advice that mostly does not work when delivered as individual exhortation. The interventions that *do* move populations are **structural**: built environments that make activity easy, food policy that makes healthful options affordable, taxation that disincentivizes harmful products. This is the recurring pattern of primary prevention — regulation outperforms willpower.

### Environment, occupation, and sun

The same regulation-beats-individual-behavior pattern governs carcinogen exposure. **Asbestos** is banned for most uses in developed countries but still used in some low-income ones, where the mesothelioma epidemic lies ahead, not behind. **Air pollution** (PM2.5) is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen addressed through emission standards, not personal choice. **Radon** — the second-leading cause of lung cancer in the U.S. after smoking — is cheap to test for and moderately expensive to mitigate, yet testing is underutilized. **Industrial chemicals** (benzene, vinyl chloride, formaldehyde) are controlled by occupational exposure limits, which work far better than asking workers to "be careful." For **UV exposure**, skin cancers are largely preventable through sun-protective behavior and avoiding tanning beds (themselves a Group 1 carcinogen); because UV-driven mutation accumulates early, childhood and adolescent sun protection is especially high-leverage.

### Chemoprevention: drugs for the healthy-but-high-risk

**Chemoprevention** is the use of drugs to prevent cancer in people at elevated risk. The evidence is strong for specific agents in specific populations:

- **Aspirin** (low-dose, long-term) reduces colorectal cancer incidence and mortality by roughly 20–30%, likely through COX-2 inhibition of inflammation-driven proliferation.
- **Tamoxifen** and **raloxifene** (selective estrogen-receptor modulators) cut invasive breast-cancer incidence by about half over five years in high-risk women — but with thromboembolic and (for tamoxifen) endometrial-cancer risks that limit uptake.
- **Aromatase inhibitors** are more effective still in postmenopausal high-risk women, with a different toxicity profile (bone loss, joint pain).
- **Finasteride** reduces prostate-cancer incidence in older men, with lingering debate about whether it prevents mostly indolent cancers.

The general pattern: chemoprevention works in identifiable high-risk populations but carries side effects that forbid broad use. The discipline is therefore *risk stratification* — finding who benefits enough to justify the harm.

### Hereditary risk: prevention for the predisposed

People with hereditary cancer syndromes face prevention in a more aggressive register. **BRCA1/2 carriers** (lifetime breast-cancer risk 65–80%, ovarian 40–60%) may choose intensive screening, chemoprevention, or prophylactic surgery (bilateral mastectomy cuts breast-cancer risk by over 90%; salpingo-oophorectomy reduces ovarian risk substantially). **Lynch syndrome** carriers undergo colonoscopy every 1–2 years from their twenties plus gynecologic surveillance. **Familial adenomatous polyposis** carries near-100% colorectal cancer risk untreated, requiring prophylactic colectomy. These decisions involve heavy trade-offs — surgery has its own consequences — and depend on genetic counseling. Multi-gene panel testing has made identifying these patients routine, and testing newly diagnosed cancer patients to find hereditary cases for family screening is increasingly standard.

## Worked Example

**Situation.** A healthy 62-year-old man with a strong family history of cardiovascular disease but average colorectal-cancer risk asks his physician whether he should take daily low-dose aspirin "to prevent cancer," having read that aspirin lowers colorectal-cancer risk. The physician must decide what to recommend.

**Reasoning — first attempt (a dead end).** The evidence is genuine: long-term low-dose aspirin reduces colorectal cancer incidence and mortality by 20–30%. Taken alone, that looks like a clear win, and the patient even has cardiovascular risk, which aspirin also addresses. The naive conclusion is "yes, start aspirin." But this treats a population-average benefit as if it applied cleanly to one healthy individual, ignoring the other arm of the ledger. Aspirin causes gastrointestinal and intracranial bleeding, and that risk rises with age — sharply in the sixties and beyond.

**Reasoning — second attempt.** The physician weighs benefit against harm *for this person at this age*. Primary prevention targets a healthy person, so the safety bar is high: you are accepting bleeding risk in someone who may never have developed cancer. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force, having previously recommended aspirin for primary prevention in some 50–69-year-olds, *narrowed* the recommendation in 2022 — no longer recommending initiation for primary prevention at age 60 or older, precisely because bleeding risk overtakes the prevention benefit in that band (USPSTF, 2022). This patient is 62 and at average cancer risk. The cardiovascular history complicates but does not automatically flip the decision, because the bleeding risk applies regardless of indication.

**Resolution.** For an average-cancer-risk 62-year-old, routine aspirin *for cancer prevention* is not recommended; the bleeding risk has caught up with the benefit. The physician individualizes the cardiovascular question separately and notes that the calculus would differ for a patient with a hereditary high-risk syndrome (e.g., Lynch syndrome), where the cancer-prevention benefit is large enough to outweigh bleeding risk.

**The lesson.** A real population-average benefit is not a personal prescription. In primary prevention, the safety bar is set by the fact that most recipients would never have gotten the disease — so benefit must be weighed against harm at the level of the individual and the age.

**The limit.** The benefit–risk line moves with new data and with better risk stratification. Genetic and biomarker-based risk scoring may eventually identify average-risk-by-history people whose true cancer risk justifies chemoprevention — so today's "no" is a calibrated judgment, not a permanent rule [contested — see pantry flag].

## Common Misconceptions

**"Prevention is nice in theory, but treatment is where the real impact on cancer mortality is."** The arithmetic says the opposite. Tobacco control alone could prevent more annual U.S. cancer deaths than every cancer drug ever developed combined, and roughly half of U.S. cancer deaths trace to modifiable factors. The opening case makes the point concrete: a $200 vaccine series prevents the very cancer that the oncology clinic is spending hundreds of thousands of dollars to treat without curing. Prevention underperforms in *funding and attention*, not in *potential impact* — a mismatch driven by how health systems pay and what patients seek, not by the underlying biology.

**"If healthful behaviors prevent cancer, the answer is to educate people to make better choices."** Education and individual exhortation are real but weak levers at population scale — people generally know smoking and obesity are harmful, and knowing changes behavior only modestly. The interventions that actually shift population cancer rates are structural: tobacco taxes, smoke-free laws, food and built-environment policy, regulation of carcinogens. Asking workers to "be careful" with benzene fails where exposure limits succeed. The misconception locates prevention in willpower; the evidence locates it in policy.

**"A drug that prevents cancer should be given to everyone to lower everyone's risk."** Chemoprevention drugs have side effects, and primary prevention is applied to mostly-healthy people who would never have developed the cancer. The worked example shows aspirin's bleeding risk overtaking its cancer benefit with age. A prevention drug is justified only where the *individual's* risk is high enough that the benefit clears the harm — which is why tamoxifen, aspirin, and finasteride are targeted to high-risk populations, not the general public. Universal application would harm more people than it helps.

## Exercises

1. **(Understand.)** Classify each of the following as primary, secondary, or tertiary prevention, and state the disease stage each targets: (a) the HPV vaccine, (b) colonoscopy in an asymptomatic 50-year-old, (c) adjuvant chemotherapy after tumor resection, (d) a tobacco tax, (e) prophylactic mastectomy in a BRCA1 carrier.

2. **(Apply.)** A public-health official has a fixed budget and must choose between funding an HPV-vaccination program and a campaign urging adults to exercise more. Using the chapter's distinction between structural and individual-behavior interventions, argue which is more likely to reduce cancer mortality per dollar, and name the single factor that most determines your answer.

3. **(Apply+.)** A 45-year-old woman is found to carry a BRCA1 mutation. Construct her primary-prevention options, the approximate risk reduction each offers, and the trade-off each imposes. Then state which option you would present first and why — and identify the piece of information about *her* (not the mutation) that would most change your recommendation.

4. **(Produce.)** Draft a one-page, risk-stratified prevention plan for a hypothetical 30-year-old with Lynch syndrome. Specify surveillance intervals, any chemoprevention with its rationale, prophylactic options and their timing, and at least one point where the plan defers a decision pending more information. Justify each element by the lifetime cancer risk it addresses.

5. **(Apply.)** Explain why the same logic that makes aspirin *not* recommended for an average-risk 62-year-old makes it potentially appropriate for a Lynch-syndrome carrier of the same age. Frame your answer in terms of where the benefit–risk line falls for each.

## What Would Change My Mind

The central claim of this chapter is that primary prevention — especially tobacco control and oncogenic-virus vaccination — is the highest-leverage way to reduce cancer mortality, far exceeding what treatment achieves per death prevented, and that it is underused for reasons of political economy rather than weak evidence. The finding that would force a revision: rigorous, long-horizon population data showing that high-coverage HPV and hepatitis B vaccination programs *failed* to reduce the incidence of their target cancers as the vaccinated cohorts aged — for instance, no decline in cervical cancer in Australia's vaccinated cohorts decades on — or evidence that comprehensive tobacco control did not lower lung-cancer mortality where smoking fell. If the marquee preventive interventions did not deliver the projected reductions, the claim that prevention out-leverages treatment would collapse, and the case for redirecting resources would weaken sharply. The early Australian and hepatitis B data point the other way, but the decisive cohorts are still aging.

## Still Puzzling

- Why does the political economy of cancer so reliably favor treatment over prevention, even when the cost-per-life-saved comparison is lopsided? Is this fixable through payment reform, or is it rooted in how patients and systems perceive dramatic versus quiet benefit?
- E-cigarettes are clearly less harmful than combusted tobacco but clearly not harmless; whether they are, on balance, a cessation tool or an initiation gateway depends on uptake patterns that differ by population. The net effect remains genuinely unsettled.
- How far can genetic and biomarker-based risk scoring extend chemoprevention's reach? If we could identify the high-risk minority within an average-risk population precisely, today's "treat only the obviously high-risk" rules might shift substantially.
- The long latency of H. pylori-driven gastric cancer and HPV-driven cancers means the full payoff of eradication and vaccination programs will not be visible for decades. We are, in effect, running experiments whose results our successors will read.
