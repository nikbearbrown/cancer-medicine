# Chapter 11 — Cancer Prevention: Stopping Cancer Before It Starts
*The Cheapest Cure Is the One That Happens Before the Disease.*

Two clinics, same building, same city. One is treating a 58-year-old woman with metastatic cervical cancer. Her care over the next two years — surgery, chemoradiation, immunotherapy, supportive care — will cost several hundred thousand dollars and, statistically, buy her time rather than a cure. Down the hall, a pediatric clinic offers the HPV vaccine to 12-year-olds. The series costs roughly $200 and prevents virtually all of the cancer the woman in oncology now has. She was never vaccinated — the vaccine didn't exist when she was twelve, and by the time it did, her county's uptake had stalled amid cost barriers and hesitancy.

The second clinic's waiting room is half empty while the first is full.

The American Cancer Society estimates that roughly half of all U.S. cancer deaths are attributable to modifiable risk factors. The WHO estimates 30–50% of the global cancer burden is preventable with tools we already have. This chapter is about where in the disease process the intervention is cheapest and most effective — and about why we so consistently underinvest there.

---

Public health sorts prevention by what stage of disease the intervention targets, and the level determines the constraints on what is acceptable to do.

**Primary prevention** stops the disease from ever occurring: not smoking, avoiding oncogenic infections, limiting carcinogen exposure, maintaining healthy weight, moderating alcohol and sun. The target is a healthy person. That target sets a severe safety requirement. You are intervening on millions of people who would never have gotten cancer anyway — the benefit to any one individual is probabilistic and small, the harm to the unlucky few who experience side effects is concrete and certain. This is why a primary prevention intervention must be extraordinarily safe and cheap. Any risk you impose on a healthy population must be justified across everyone you treat, not just the minority you help.

**Secondary prevention** detects disease at an early, more curable stage — mammography, colonoscopy, low-dose CT for lung cancer, Pap smears. The target is an asymptomatic person at elevated risk. The test must reliably separate people who have cancer from people who don't; false positives lead to invasive follow-up, false negatives to missed diagnoses.

**Tertiary prevention** manages established disease to prevent complications and progression — essentially the rest of oncology.

The recurring policy error is confusing the levels: accepting treatment-grade risks in healthy people, or being so cautious about healthy-population safety that an effective high-risk intervention gets withheld. The level tells you the constraint.

If you wanted to save the most lives from cancer with a single policy lever, the answer is tobacco control — and has been for seventy years.

Tobacco is the dominant modifiable cause of cancer worldwide: about 22% of cancer deaths globally, roughly 30% in high-income countries. The causal case was settled by Richard Doll and Austin Bradford Hill's British Doctors Study, a prospective cohort that enrolled 40,000 British physicians in 1951 and followed them for decades (Doll & Hill, 1954). Before it, the smoking–lung-cancer link rested on case-control data that skeptics could dismiss as confounded. A prospective cohort in which smokers' lung-cancer deaths accumulated in a dose-dependent pattern, with the dose carefully measured at enrollment before any outcome was known, was not easily dismissed. Tobacco causes cancer of the lung, oral cavity, larynx, esophagus, pancreas, bladder, kidney, cervix, and acute myeloid leukemia. The mechanism is not subtle — tobacco smoke contains dozens of characterized carcinogens that produce specific patterns of mutation, including the G→T transversions that dominate the mutational signature of smokers' lung cancers.

The scale of the opportunity is hard to hold in your head. In the U.S., lung cancer kills about 140,000 people a year, roughly 90% tobacco-related. Eliminating tobacco would prevent more annual cancer deaths than every cancer therapy ever developed, combined. That is not rhetoric. It is arithmetic.

Primary prevention of tobacco-driven cancer operates at three points. **Preventing initiation** in young people — the intervention must reach adolescents, because almost no one begins smoking as an adult. High tobacco taxes are among the most consistently effective tools ever studied in public health: they raise the barrier to experimentation, and experimentation is where addiction usually starts. Advertising restrictions, plain packaging, age-of-sale limits, and smoke-free public spaces work through overlapping mechanisms. **Promoting cessation** in current users — counseling combined with pharmacotherapy (nicotine replacement, varenicline, bupropion) raises quit rates substantially compared to willpower alone; brief physician advice to quit, even without extended follow-up, measurably increases cessation and takes about thirty seconds. Most primary-care visits still skip it. **Harm reduction** in people who will not quit — switching from combusted tobacco to non-combusted nicotine products reduces exposure to carcinogens; the benefit is real but the population-level net effect depends on whether harm reduction functions as cessation assistance or as a gateway to new users, and that question is genuinely unresolved for e-cigarettes.

---

About 13% of cancer globally is caused by infection, and most of that fraction is preventable by vaccination or by eliminating the infection. This is the other large primary-prevention opportunity, and the one with the most dramatic recent evidence.

**HPV vaccination** is the most powerful cancer-prevention vaccine ever developed. The current 9-valent vaccine targets the major oncogenic HPV types responsible for essentially all cervical cancer, the majority of anal cancers, and substantial fractions of oropharyngeal, vulvar, vaginal, and penile cancers. It works best before first exposure — hence the recommendation for ages 11–12 — and in countries with high coverage, the prevalence of vaccine-targeted HPV types in young women has fallen 80–90%. Australia introduced universal adolescent vaccination in 2007. Cervical cancer incidence in vaccinated cohorts is falling, and Australia is projected to effectively eliminate cervical cancer as a public-health problem within the coming decades. That is a disease that kills hundreds of thousands of women globally each year, being eliminated by a vaccine that costs a fraction of what a single course of chemotherapy costs, from a country that decided to cover it universally and achieved high uptake. The barriers in countries that have not are access, cost, and hesitancy — none of them biological.

**Hepatitis B vaccination** has been available since 1982. It is called "the first cancer-preventing vaccine" for good reason: it prevents most HBV-driven hepatocellular carcinoma, which kills about 700,000 people a year globally. Its integration into routine infant immunization schedules is one of the great public-health achievements of the past forty years. The full payoff is still arriving — the cancers prevented are those that would have developed decades after infections that never occurred — but the epidemiological signal is clear in vaccinated populations.

**Hepatitis C** has no vaccine but is now curable. Direct-acting antivirals achieve cure rates above 95%, and treating the infection substantially reduces subsequent liver-cancer risk. **Helicobacter pylori** eradication with a short antibiotic course prevents gastric cancer — Japan implemented a national eradication program in 2013, though the long latency between infection and cancer means the full effect will take decades to read.

The pattern across all of these: an infection that causes cancer through decades of chronic inflammation and genomic damage can be interrupted before that damage accumulates. The window is large; the tools are inexpensive; the barriers are organizational and political.

---

Body weight, physical activity, diet, and alcohol each contribute meaningfully to cancer risk, and the temptation is to present them as a list of things individuals should do differently. That temptation leads to policy that consistently underperforms.

**Obesity** is causally linked to at least 13 cancers — endometrial, postmenopausal breast, colorectal, liver, kidney, pancreatic, thyroid, among others — and is the second-ranking modifiable risk factor after tobacco in high-income countries, where smoking rates have fallen. The mechanism involves adipose tissue's endocrine function: excess adipose secretes estrogens, insulin, IGF-1, and pro-inflammatory adipokines that drive cell proliferation and survival. **Physical activity** reduces the risk of colorectal, postmenopausal breast, and endometrial cancer through multiple mechanisms including reduced circulating insulin and estrogens, reduced inflammation, and gut motility effects. **Diet** matters at the pattern level — Mediterranean and DASH-style diets associate with lower cancer mortality — and at the component level: processed meat is an IARC Group 1 carcinogen for colorectal cancer, not because of a single chemical but because of heme iron, nitrosamines, and the bacterial metabolism of high-protein loads in the colon. **Alcohol** causes cancers of the mouth, throat, esophagus, liver, breast, and colorectum, through acetaldehyde-driven DNA damage and estrogen elevation, with a dose-response that has no clearly safe floor for breast cancer specifically.

Here is the honest assessment of what works. Telling people to eat better, move more, and drink less is biologically correct advice that mostly does not move populations. It is not that individuals cannot change behavior — they can, and some do. It is that exhortation is a weak lever at scale. The interventions that actually shift population-level cancer rates are structural: built environments that make physical activity the default rather than the effort; food systems that make healthful options affordable rather than expensive; taxation and labeling that change the economics of harmful products. Tobacco taxes, not anti-smoking lectures, drove the most dramatic declines in smoking rates. The same principle governs everything else. Regulation outperforms willpower at population scale — reliably, repeatedly, across different countries, behaviors, and cancers.

---

**Chemoprevention** is the use of drugs to prevent cancer in people at elevated risk. It is the part of primary prevention that most resembles clinical medicine — specific agents, specific doses, specific risk populations — and it illustrates the benefit-risk calculus of primary prevention most sharply.

The evidence for selected agents is strong. **Aspirin** (low-dose, long-term) reduces colorectal cancer incidence and mortality by roughly 20–30%, likely through COX-2 inhibition of inflammation-driven proliferation in pre-malignant adenomas. **Tamoxifen** and **raloxifene**, selective estrogen-receptor modulators, cut invasive breast-cancer incidence by about half over five years in high-risk women. **Aromatase inhibitors** are more effective still in postmenopausal women at high risk, trading the thromboembolic risk of tamoxifen for bone loss and joint pain. **Finasteride** reduces prostate-cancer incidence in older men, with debate about whether it mostly prevents indolent cancers that would not have caused harm.

The hard constraint in each case: side effects that are acceptable in someone facing serious cancer risk are not acceptable in someone who would probably never have gotten cancer. This constraint produces the defining discipline of chemoprevention — **risk stratification**. The drug is appropriate only where the individual's cancer risk is high enough that the prevention benefit clearly outweighs the drug's harms. For people with hereditary cancer syndromes, primary prevention operates in a more aggressive register, because the cancer risk is not statistical — it is near-certain over a lifetime.

**BRCA1/2 carriers** face lifetime breast-cancer risks of 65–80% and ovarian risks of 40–60%. Their prevention options span a spectrum: intensive surveillance (annual MRI plus mammography, starting at 25–30); chemoprevention (tamoxifen or risk-reducing oophorectomy to suppress estrogen); and prophylactic surgery — bilateral mastectomy cutting breast-cancer risk by over 90%, salpingo-oophorectomy substantially reducing ovarian risk and also lowering breast-cancer risk if done premenopausally. Prophylactic oophorectomy has its own consequences: surgical menopause, bone-density loss, cardiovascular effects. These are not small trade-offs. They are accepted when the alternative is a near-certainty of cancer.

**Lynch syndrome** carriers — who have germline mutations in mismatch-repair genes — face elevated risks of colorectal, endometrial, ovarian, gastric, and other cancers. Management involves colonoscopy every 1–2 years beginning in the mid-twenties, gynecologic surveillance, and in some cases aspirin chemoprevention, where the Lynch-syndrome-specific risk is high enough to shift the benefit-risk balance relative to average-risk individuals. **Familial adenomatous polyposis** — hundreds to thousands of colorectal adenomas beginning in adolescence, near-100% colorectal cancer risk untreated — requires prophylactic colectomy. There is no chemoprevention option that manages that polyp burden; the colon must come out.

The infrastructure for identifying these patients has improved substantially. Multi-gene panel testing is routine for newly diagnosed cancer patients, and germline findings prompt family cascade testing — converting the patient's diagnosis into an opportunity to prevent cancer in relatives who are still in the primary-prevention window.

---

A healthy 62-year-old man with a strong family history of cardiovascular disease asks his physician whether he should take daily low-dose aspirin for cancer prevention. He has read that aspirin lowers colorectal cancer risk, and that is true.

The naive reasoning: aspirin reduces colorectal cancer incidence and mortality by 20–30%. This patient has cardiovascular risk too. Start aspirin. But this treats a population-average benefit as a personal prescription and ignores the other side of the ledger. Aspirin causes gastrointestinal and intracranial bleeding, and that risk rises with age — steeply in the sixties and beyond.

Work through it more carefully. Primary prevention targets a healthy person. The safety requirement is therefore severe: you are accepting a concrete bleeding risk in someone who may never have developed cancer. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force previously recommended aspirin for primary prevention in some 50–69-year-olds. In 2022 it narrowed that recommendation — no longer recommending initiation for primary prevention in people 60 or older — because the bleeding risk has, by that age, grown large enough to overtake the cancer-prevention benefit for an average-risk individual (USPSTF, 2022). This patient is 62 and at average cancer risk.

The right answer for cancer prevention, for this patient, at this age: no aspirin. The cardiovascular question is separate and handled by cardiology guidelines on their own terms.

Now shift the patient slightly. Same age, 62, but Lynch syndrome. The colorectal-cancer prevention benefit of aspirin in Lynch syndrome is substantially larger than in the general population — the cancer risk is high enough, and the mechanism of benefit is well-matched to the syndrome's biology, that the benefit-risk line falls in a different place. The aspirin that is inappropriate for an average-risk 62-year-old may be appropriate for a Lynch-syndrome carrier of the same age. Same drug, same dose, same age, different answer — because the benefit depends on the individual risk that the drug is offsetting.

The lesson generalizes. A population-average effect is not a personal prescription. In primary prevention, the math must be done at the individual level, and the denominator is everyone you would treat — including all the people who would never have gotten cancer — not just the fraction you help.

---

The pattern of underinvestment in prevention is not mysterious once you understand how health systems pay for things. A dramatic, late intervention — surgery, chemotherapy, a targeted drug — is billed at the moment it is delivered, attributed to a specific patient, and visible as a saved life or a bought year. A quiet intervention — a tax, a vaccine, a smoke-free law — prevents a disease that was going to happen to an anonymous person twenty years from now. The prevented case never appears on any ledger. The health system that fails to prevent it still gets paid when it treats it.

This is not a defect in the biology of prevention. The biology is settled. Tobacco control, HPV and HBV vaccination, H. pylori eradication, carcinogen regulation — these are among the most carefully studied and best-understood interventions in all of medicine, and their population-level effects are large. The gap between what is known and what is done is political economy, not science.

The two clinics at the start of this chapter are still there. The oncology clinic's waiting room is full. The pediatric clinic's waiting room is half empty. The most important insight this chapter offers is not which drugs prevent cancer — it is why the arithmetic so clearly favors the second clinic, and why the first one keeps getting all the resources.

*What this tests: precise application of the three-level framework; ability to distinguish the target stage from the intervention's intensity.*

2. *[Recall — moderate]* Explain in three sentences why primary prevention must meet a higher safety standard than tertiary prevention, using the denominator — who receives the intervention — as the core of your argument.
*What this tests: understanding that the acceptable risk of an intervention is set by the population it is applied to, not by the mechanism of the intervention.*

3. *[Recall — moderate]* Describe the key feature of Doll and Hill's 1954 British Doctors Study that made it more convincing evidence for smoking causality than the case-control studies that preceded it. Name the design element and explain why it addressed the main objection to earlier data.

**Application**

*What this tests: applying the structural-versus-individual-behavior distinction to a resource-allocation decision; recognizing that biological efficacy and population-level effectiveness can diverge.*

5. *[Apply — moderate-hard]* A 45-year-old woman with a newly identified BRCA1 mutation comes to discuss her options. 6. *[Apply — hard]* Explain why the same reasoning that makes aspirin not recommended for primary cancer prevention in an average-risk 62-year-old makes it potentially appropriate for a Lynch syndrome carrier of the same age. *What this tests: applying the benefit-risk logic of chemoprevention to two populations with the same drug; understanding that individual risk level, not just drug properties, determines the recommendation.*

**Synthesis**

7. *[Synthesis — hard]* Draft a risk-stratified prevention plan for a 30-year-old newly identified Lynch syndrome carrier. Specify surveillance intervals and the rationale for each, any chemoprevention options with the cancer type they address, prophylactic surgical options and their appropriate timing, and at least one point where the plan defers a decision pending more information. Justify each element by reference to the specific cancer risk it addresses and the level of prevention it represents.
*What this tests: integrating multiple prevention modalities for a high-risk individual; applying level-appropriate risk-benefit reasoning across primary and secondary prevention simultaneously.*

8. *[Synthesis — hard]* The chapter claims that structural interventions — tobacco taxes, food policy, built-environment design — outperform individual-behavior interventions at population scale for preventing cancer. *What this tests: honest engagement with the limits of the structural-intervention argument; ability to identify when individual-level approaches work and why they usually don't scale.*

**Challenge**

## What Would Change My Mind

The central claim of this chapter is that primary prevention — especially tobacco control and oncogenic-virus vaccination — is the highest-leverage way to reduce cancer mortality, far exceeding what treatment achieves per death prevented, and that it is underused for reasons of political economy rather than weak evidence. The finding that would force a revision: rigorous, long-horizon population data showing that high-coverage HPV and hepatitis B vaccination programs failed to reduce the incidence of their target cancers as vaccinated cohorts aged — no decline in cervical cancer in Australia's vaccinated cohorts decades on, or no fall in hepatocellular carcinoma in populations with near-universal infant HBV vaccination. If the marquee preventive interventions did not deliver the projected reductions, the claim that prevention out-leverages treatment would collapse, and the case for redirecting resources would weaken sharply. The early Australian and hepatitis B data point the other way, but the decisive cohorts are still aging.

## Still Puzzling

- Why does the political economy of cancer so reliably favor treatment over prevention, even when the cost-per-life-saved comparison is lopsided? Is this fixable through payment reform, or is it rooted in how patients and systems perceive dramatic versus quiet benefit?
- E-cigarettes are clearly less harmful than combusted tobacco but clearly not harmless. Whether they are, on balance, a cessation tool or an initiation gateway depends on uptake patterns that differ by population. The net effect on population-level cancer risk remains genuinely unsettled.
- How far can genetic and biomarker-based risk scoring extend chemoprevention's reach? If we could identify the high-risk minority within an average-risk population precisely, today's conservative recommendations might shift substantially.
- The long latency of H. pylori-driven gastric cancer and HPV-driven cancers means the full payoff of eradication and vaccination programs will not be visible for decades. We are running experiments whose results our successors will read.
